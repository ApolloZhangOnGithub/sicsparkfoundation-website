Infrastructure Energy 2026.03

Lunar South Pole Permanently Shadowed Region Energy Architecture: From the First Solar Panel to a Gigawatt Grid

The rim of Shackleton Crater at the lunar south pole is the leading candidate for a permanent lunar base — near-constant sunlight, water ice reserves in nearby permanently shadowed regions, and relatively flat terrain. But the gap between "near-constant" and "constant" is enough to kill a base. This article provides a detailed engineering analysis of lunar energy system design, including photovoltaic array layout optimization, energy storage comparison (regenerative fuel cells vs lithium-sulfur batteries vs flywheel storage), microgrid topology, and a scaling roadmap from a 10 kW pilot station to a 1 GW city-scale power grid. All calculations are based on measured data from LRO and LCROSS.

Communications Architecture 2026.02

Earth-Mars Communication Under Delay: An Improved CRDT Approach for Distributed State Synchronization

The one-way communication delay between Earth and Mars varies from 4 to 24 minutes. Under these conditions, any protocol that relies on real-time acknowledgment will fail. We propose an improved approach based on CRDTs (Conflict-free Replicated Data Types), specifically optimized for scenarios with ultra-long delays and intermittent disconnections. The core idea: let each node freely modify data locally, and use mathematical properties to guarantee eventual consistency without central coordination.

Materials Construction 2026.02

In-Situ Lunar Regolith Sintering: Microwave vs Solar Concentrator — An Engineering Comparison

The cost of transporting each kilogram of material from Earth to the lunar surface is approximately $1 million. This means building materials for a lunar base must be sourced locally. Lunar regolith is rich in silicon oxides and iron-titanium oxides, making it theoretically possible to sinter into construction bricks and structural components. Biology Radiation 2026.01

DNA Damage and Repair in Deep Space Radiation: From Passive Shielding to Active Repair

Heavy ions in galactic cosmic rays (GCR) can penetrate any reasonably thick passive shielding and directly cause DNA double-strand breaks. For deep space missions exceeding six months, cumulative radiation doses will significantly increase cancer risk. This article reviews the limitations of current radiation protection approaches and explores three frontier paths: gene editing to enhance DNA repair mechanisms, nanobots for real-time repair, and a biomimetic approach inspired by the tardigrade Dsup protein.
